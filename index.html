Stark shift

Pedersen, “Exciton Stark Shift and Electroabsorption in Monolayer Transition-Metal Dichalcogenides.”

\[\begin{aligned} \alpha & = 3.5r_0^{1.8}\quad\textrm{for }r_0\gg 1\\ \end{aligned} \]

The Stark shift can then be calculated by \[\begin{aligned} Δ E & \approx -\frac{1}{2}\alpha F^2\\ \end{aligned} \]

Stark shift

Xu et al., “Electronic and Optical Properties of the Monolayer Group-IV Monochalcogenides $MX$ ($M=\mathrm{Ge},\phantom{\rule{4pt}{0ex}}\mathrm{Sn}$; $X=\mathrm{S},\phantom{\rule{4pt}{0ex}}\mathrm{Se},\phantom{\rule{4pt}{0ex}}\mathrm{Te}$).”

\[\begin{aligned} r_0 & = L \frac{\epsilon -1}{2}\quad \textrm{(Cudazzo et al.)}\\ \end{aligned} \]

∴ slope of ε v.s. 1/L plot = 2r0

By calculating the ε at different interlayer distances L, one can determine r0.

Calculated polarizability

slope of ε v.s. 1/L plot = 2r0

∴ r0 = 21.2 Å = 39.97 a.u.

α ≈ 3.5 r01.8 = 2674 a.u. = 2.754 x 10-4 meV (μm/V)2

Stark shift & Exciton Dissociation

Massicotte et al., “Dissociation of Two-Dimensional Excitons in Monolayer WSe2.”

Compared to TMDs, few-layers InSe binding energies are an order of magnitude smaller (≈ 20 meV v.s. hundreds of meV)

In-plane polarizability of InSe excitons could possibly be larger than TMDs. Stark shift might be observable at low fields.

Polarizability can be calculated when one knows the screening length r0 of the system.

Stark shift & Exciton Dissociation

Massicotte et al., “Dissociation of Two-Dimensional Excitons in Monolayer WSe2.”

Additionaly, measuring the photoresponse rate allows one to determine if the system is in weak-field or high-field regime i.e. dissociation rate-limited or drift rate limited.

I think photoresponse rate can be extracted from the decay tail of the signal pulse as observed in an oscilloscope.
